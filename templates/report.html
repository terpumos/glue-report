1. Introduction

A fast regulatory intervention in case of traffic incidents minimizes potential economic damage. Therefore an adequate placement of traffic inspectors is paramount for traffic regulation authorities. This study investigates an optimal distribution of traffic inspectors in the Netherlands based on a dataset of traffic incidents of the years 2010 - 2015.

2. Incident Density

In order to make regions classifiable, the area covered by this study was subdivided on a Gaussian grid, with a grid spacing of 0.06Â° along the latitude axis and 0.1Â° along the longitude. The relative deviation of surface covered by the grid point furthest south to the grid point furthest in the north is 7.1%, which is compensated by examining the incidents per area. The representation of all grid points with at least one incident is covered on the interactive graph "Traffic Incidents Hotspots".

The distribution of incidents reveals the location of highways and population centers. A recommendation for the placement of 80 traffic inspectors can be obtained by switching the selection on the graph to "Top 80 Hotspots".

3. Working Day - Weekend Influences

The interactive graph "Incident Daytime Distribution" covers the time distribution of incidents for weekends and working days.

An average working day shows two peaks marking the rush hours in the morning and evening. An average weekend day reveals an increase of incidents with a single peak at noon and a following decrease. Even though the total numbers of incidents per hour during the night are higher on weekends, working days have a significantly higher total rate of incidents. Due to a 5 day work week of most people, a variation between incident distributions for working days and the weekend can be expected. However this hypothesis is not supported by interactive graph "Incidents on Weekends/Working Days". Differences of incident hotspots between weekends and working days are rather small and focus on areas with a low number of incidents.

4. Daytime Impact on Incident Locations

As seen in the "Incident Daytime Distribution" graphic the likelihood of an incident strongly depends on the given daytime. A further investigation of the inspector's driving time (time between the assignment of an incident and the arrival at incident sight), occupation time (time between assignment and operation finishing) and waiting time (time between incident notification and assignment) is made in graphic "Incident Response Time". The occupation time and driving time show a strong dependency to the time of the day, with a peak in the early morning hours. A possible explanation for this difference is a higher number of incidents at daytime, resulting in in increase of time pressure. The waiting time shows three peaks, of whom two can be explained by the rush hours.

The previous findings suggest that a time of day location dependency might exist. This assumption can be studied in graph "Incident Hotspots at Given Daytime ".
The location of the grid cells with the highest numbers of incidents is relative stable while a fluctuation in the ranking of grid cells with lower numbers of incidents can be observed.

5. Most Significant Findings

The most significant findings of this study are:

  • The highest spots of incident density the urban centers of:

    1. Rotterdam
    2. Amsterdam
    3. Eindhoven
    4. The Hague

  • The weakest points of the intensity range are about 4 times smaller than the strongest ones.

  • Time distribution of incidents between weekends and working days is different: Weekends show a single peak at 15:00, while working days have two peaks at the rush hours of 08:00 and 17:00.

  • Inspectors spend more time at the incident sights at night (01:00-07:00, ~1h 25min) comparing to noon (~45 min).
